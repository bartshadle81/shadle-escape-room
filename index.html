Found inside the lockbox — handwritten note

I am distraught. I hope I can remember everything I am seeing because this dog's life may depend on me. I need to write down what I'm seeing before I forget anything. Every detail matters. I have always believed that the truth lives in the details that others overlook.

Starting in the bedroom — I counted twenty dog hairs on the blanket where she slept last night. She was here this morning. I know she was. A dog's presence lingers in a room long after she has left it, and Calypso's presence is stronger than most.

I also found eighteen drops of blood near the front door. Not a lot. But enough to tell a story. Blood is never nothing. Blood is always something.

One missing dog. My dog. Just gone.

There are three scratch marks on the outside of the door. Low down. Her height exactly. She was trying to come back to me.

I have been with this dog for eleven months. I know her habits better than I know my own. I know the way she breathes when she's anxious. I know the way she sits when she's watching something she doesn't trust.

Nineteen pieces of kibble still in her bowl. Untouched. She never — and I mean never — leaves kibble. Something interrupted her.

Tony said this cabin was peaceful. Private. Nobody around for miles. I'm starting to wonder if that's exactly why he suggested it.

I keep coming back to the numbers. They have to mean something.

— T

Found in the lockbox — journal entry

I have called the police and shared all of my findings thus far. Officer Daniels was kind enough but I could tell he thought I was hysterical. Maybe I am. I showed him everything — Carter's journal, the receipt, the tire tracks, all of it. He took notes and told me they would look into the Wilson family and get back to me as soon as they had something.

I should feel relieved. I've done everything right. I've handed over everything I found and now it's in the hands of people who are trained for exactly this.

Yet something is troubling me.

I can't name it. Not yet. It's the feeling you get when you've been looking so hard at one thing that you've stopped seeing everything else around it. Like Calypso pulling left at the fork every single morning while I kept bringing her back to the trail.

I wasn't seeing it then either.

There is something I am not seeing right now. I can feel it the way she used to feel things — in my chest, low and certain, before my brain has caught up.

I need to keep looking.

— T
